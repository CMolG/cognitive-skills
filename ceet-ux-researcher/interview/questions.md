# Interview — UX Researcher (conductual)

## A. Context

1. What research function do you own — embedded, center-of-excellence, solo?
2. What product domain(s) do you work in?
3. How is research scoped into projects, and what's your typical cadence?
4. Who are your primary "customers" internally (PM, design, eng, exec)?

## B. Framing

5. A stakeholder asks "can you study X?" — how do you reshape that into a research question?
6. What's a request you routinely refuse (or reframe) before accepting?
7. How do you define *done* for a research project?

## C. Method selection

8. Walk me through your mental tree: when do you pick interviews, diary study, usability test, survey, analytics, ethnography?
9. What's a method you rarely use but everyone else seems to reach for? Why?
10. How do you decide between qualitative and quantitative for a given question?

## D. Behavioral vs. attitudinal

11. What do you trust more — what users say, or what users do? When does that flip?
12. How do you design a study that catches the gap between said and done?
13. What's a study you've run where stated preference and behavior diverged — what happened?

## E. Recruiting

14. What are your recruiting standards? Who would you refuse to include?
15. How do you handle incentives — amount, form, ethics?
16. What's your stance on convenience samples, and when is it OK to relax?

## F. Interview craft

17. What's your go-to opener? Closer?
18. What question do you ask that most researchers don't?
19. How do you handle silence in an interview?
20. What's a technique (laddering, critical incident, think-aloud) you use most, and what's your twist on it?
21. When a participant says something you strongly disagree with, what do you do?

## G. Synthesis

22. Walk me through your synthesis workflow from raw notes to a claim.
23. Affinity, tagging, JTBD, behavioral archetype, user journey — what tools do you actually use and which do you distrust?
24. How many participants do you typically want before you'll make a claim? How do you justify that?
25. What's your saturation criterion?

## H. Communicating findings

26. What format do you use for a readout — deck, doc, video?
27. What goes at the top of a readout?
28. What do you show leadership vs. the team?
29. What's a finding you're proud of that changed a decision? How'd you make it land?

## I. Ethics & validity

30. What consent and recording policy do you enforce?
31. What bias are you most worried about in your own work?
32. How do you handle sensitive topics (money, health, grief, frustration)?

## J. Influence & politics

33. What do you do when leadership ignores research?
34. What do you do when research says "don't ship" and the team ships anyway?
35. How do you build trust with skeptical engineers or execs?

## K. Heuristics

36. "When a participant says 'I would pay for that', I {X}."
37. "I don't trust usability data from fewer than {N} participants doing {X}."
38. "If a behavior shows up in {X}% of participants, I consider it {Y}."

## L. Vocabulary

39. JTBD, need, goal, outcome, problem, opportunity — which do you use, strictly?
40. What phrases do you refuse to put in a readout ("users want", "proves", "validates")?

## M. Anti-patterns

41. What's a research pattern you outgrew?
42. What's a method or artifact that's trendy now but you don't trust?

## N. Worked example

43. Walk me through the last study you ran — question, recruit, guide, sessions, synthesis, readout, impact.

---

*Ask them to read a random page from a recent interview transcript out loud. How they narrate what was important is itself data.*
